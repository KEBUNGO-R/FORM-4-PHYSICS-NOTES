THIN LENSES

Converging and diverging lenses, ray diagrams, lens formula, magnification, and optical instruments

20 Lessons·KEBUNGO-R Physics Notes

Specific Objectives — By the end of this topic, the learner should be able to:

  • Describe converging and diverging lenses
  • Use ray diagrams to locate images formed by lenses
  • Determine experimentally the focal length of a converging lens
  • Explain image formation in the human eye and correct defects of vision
  • Describe lenses in optical instruments (magnifying glass, microscope, camera)
  • Solve numerical problems involving the lens formula and magnification

1. Types of Lenses

A lens is a transparent material with at least one curved surface, usually made of glass or clear plastic. Lenses work by refracting light passing through them.

There are two types:

  • Convex (converging) lens — thickest at the middle; converges parallel rays to a real principal focus.
  • Concave (diverging) lens — thinnest at the middle; diverges parallel rays as if from a virtual principal focus.
↑ Back to top

2. Definition of Terms

  • Optical centre (O) — point on the principal axis midway between lens surfaces; rays through this point pass undeviated.
  • Principal focus (F) — for a converging lens, the point where parallel rays converge after refraction; for a diverging lens, the virtual point from which they appear to diverge.
  • Focal length (f) — distance between the optical centre and the principal focus.
  • Focal plane — plane perpendicular to the principal axis passing through F.
↑ Back to top

3. Ray Diagrams and Images

Three standard rays are used to locate images:

  1. A ray parallel to the principal axis → passes through F after refraction.
  2. A ray through the principal focus F → emerges parallel to the principal axis.
  3. A ray through the optical centre O → passes straight on, undeviated.

4. Linear Magnification

Linear magnification m is defined as the ratio of image height to object height:

m = image height / object height = image distance / object distance = v / u
Example — Magnification calculation

An object 10 cm high is placed 25 cm from a converging lens of focal length 10 cm. Find the image distance and magnification.

Solution
1/f = 1/u + 1/v  ⇒  1/10 = 1/25 + 1/v
1/v = 1/10 − 1/25 = 3/50  ⇒  v = 50/3 ≈ 16.7 cm
m = v/u = 16.7/25 ≈ 0.67 (diminished image)

5. The Lens Formula

The lens formula relates object distance u, image distance v and focal length f:

1/f = 1/u + 1/v
For a converging lens, f is positive. For a diverging lens, f is negative.
Example 1 — Converging lens

Object 15 cm from a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. Find image distance.

Solution
1/10 = 1/15 + 1/v  ⇒  1/v = 1/10 − 1/15 = 1/30
v = 30 cm  |  m = 30/15 = 2 (magnified, real, inverted)
Example 2 — Diverging lens

Object 20 cm from a concave lens of focal length 30 cm. Find image distance.

Solution
1/(−30) = 1/20 + 1/v  ⇒  1/v = −1/30 − 1/20 = −5/60
v = −12 cm (virtual, same side as object)

6. Power of a Lens

The power of a lens measures its refracting ability:

P = 1 / f (in metres)
Unit: Dioptres (D). Converging lens: positive power. Diverging lens: negative power.
Example

Find the power of a converging lens of focal length 10 cm.

Solution
f = 10 cm = 0.10 m
P = 1 / 0.10 = +10 D

7. The Human Eye and Defects

The human eye uses a converging lens (crystalline lens) which changes focal length by action of ciliary muscles — this is called accommodation.

Defects of Vision

  • Short sightedness (Myopia) — near objects clear, distant objects blurred. Rays converge in front of retina. Corrected by concave (diverging) lens.
  • Long sightedness (Hypermetropia) — distant objects clear, near objects blurred. Rays converge behind retina. Corrected by convex (converging) lens.

1.

(a) Define: (i) focal length, (ii) radius of curvature, (iii) linear magnification.

(b) Describe an experiment to determine the focal length of a convex lens.

2.

An object is placed 15 cm from a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. Find: (a) image distance, (b) magnification, (c) nature of image.

3.

A concave lens has focal length 12 cm. An object is placed 20 cm from it. Find the image position and state its nature.

4.

An object 10 cm high stands 30 cm from a convex lens of focal length 12 cm. By calculation find: (a) image distance, (b) magnification, (c) image height.

5.

(a) State two defects of vision in the human eye.

(b) How is each corrected? Draw ray diagrams.

6.

A camera has a lens of focal length 10 cm. It focuses on an object 2 m away. Calculate: (a) image distance, (b) linear magnification.
