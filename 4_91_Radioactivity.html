1. Atomic Structure and Radioactivity

Radioactivity is the spontaneous, random disintegration of unstable atomic nuclei with emission of particles or radiation.

The proton number (Z) is the number of protons; the nucleon number (A) is the total of protons + neutrons.

Stable nuclei have a proton-to-neutron ratio of about 1:1. Unstable nuclei undergo radioactive decay to achieve stability.

3. Radioactive Decay and Nuclear Equations

Alpha Decay

AZX → A−4Z−2Y + 42He
Mass number decreases by 4, proton number decreases by 2

Beta Decay

AZX → AZ+1Y + 0−1e
Mass number unchanged, proton number increases by 1

Gamma Emission

Gamma rays are emitted when a nucleus loses energy after alpha or beta decay. No change in mass number or proton number.

Example — Nuclear equation

Uranium-238 undergoes alpha decay. Write the nuclear equation.

Solution
23892U → AZY + 42He
A = 238 − 4 = 234  |  Z = 92 − 2 = 90
23892U → 23490Th + 42He
The daughter is Thorium-234

4. Half-Life and Decay Law

The half-life (t½) is the time taken for half the radioactive nuclei in a sample to decay. It is constant for a given nuclide and unaffected by external conditions.

N = N0 × (1/2)n
where N = remaining nuclei, N0 = initial nuclei, n = number of half-lives
n = total time / t½
Activity A = λN    where λ = decay constant = ln2 / t½
Example — Half-life calculation

A radioactive sample has a half-life of 4 days. Starting with 640 g, how much remains after 20 days?

Solution
Number of half-lives: n = 20 / 4 = 5
N = N0 × (1/2)5 = 640 × 1/32 = 20 g

5. Nuclear Fission and Fusion

Nuclear Fission

A heavy nucleus splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing large amounts of energy and 2–3 neutrons. These neutrons can trigger further fissions — a chain reaction.

23592U + 10n → 14156Ba + 9236Kr + 310n + energy
Used in: nuclear power stations, atomic bombs

Nuclear Fusion

Two light nuclei join to form a heavier nucleus, releasing enormous energy. Requires extremely high temperatures (~107 K).

21H + 31H → 42He + 10n + energy
Used in: hydrogen bombs; research in tokamak reactors
Energy source: In both fission and fusion, a small mass is converted to energy according to Einstein’s equation: E = mc²

6. Hazards and Applications

Hazards: Ionising radiation damages living cells and DNA, causing radiation sickness, cancer, and genetic mutations.

Precautions

  • Store radioactive sources in lead-lined containers
  • Use tongs to handle sources (never bare hands)
  • Keep sources pointed away from people
  • Work behind lead screens
  • Workers wear dosimeters to monitor exposure
