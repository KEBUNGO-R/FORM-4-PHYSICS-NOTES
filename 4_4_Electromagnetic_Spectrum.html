ELECTROMAGNETIC SPECTRUM

Properties, classification, detection, applications and hazards of electromagnetic waves

15 Lessons·KEBUNGO-R Physics Notes

Specific Objectives — By the end of this topic, the learner should be able to:

  • Describe the complete electromagnetic spectrum in order of wavelength and frequency
  • State the properties common to all electromagnetic waves
  • Identify sources and detectors of each type of EM radiation
  • Describe applications of EM radiations including the greenhouse effect
  • State hazards of some EM radiations and precautions
  • Solve numerical problems using c = fλ

1. Properties of Electromagnetic Waves

Electromagnetic (EM) waves are transverse waves resulting from oscillating electric and magnetic fields at right angles to each other. Light, radio waves, X-rays, UV, IR, and gamma rays are all EM waves.

All EM waves share these properties:

  • Travel through a vacuum at the speed of light: c = 3.0 × 108 m s−1
  • Do not require a medium for transmission (can travel through vacuum)
  • Are transverse waves (can be plane-polarised)
  • Obey the wave equation: c = fλ
  • Carry energy and travel in straight lines

3. Hazards of EM Waves

Hazardous EM waves: UV, X-rays, and gamma rays carry enough energy to ionise atoms and damage living tissue.
  • Ultraviolet — causes sunburn, skin cancer, eye damage. Protection: sunscreen, UV-blocking glasses.
  • X-rays & Gamma rays — penetrate tissue, damage cells and DNA, cause cancer. Protection: lead shielding, minimum exposure time.
  • Microwaves — heat body tissue; mobile phones may pose health risks with prolonged use.
  • Greenhouse effect — IR radiation from Earth’s surface absorbed by CO&sub2; and other greenhouse gases, leading to global warming.
