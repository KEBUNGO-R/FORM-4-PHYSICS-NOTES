FLOATING AND SINKING

Archimedes' principle, law of flotation, relative density, and applications

15 Lessons·KEBUNGO-R Physics Notes

Specific Objectives — By the end of this topic, the learner should be able to:

  • State and verify Archimedes' principle
  • State the law of flotation
  • Define relative density and use it in calculations
  • Describe applications of Archimedes' principle (ships, submarines, balloons, hydrometer)
  • Solve numerical problems involving upthrust and relative density

1. Archimedes' Principle

Archimedes’ principle states: when a body is partially or totally immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upthrust equal to the weight of the fluid displaced.

Upthrust U = weight of fluid displaced
U = Vρg
where V = volume submerged, ρ = fluid density, g = 10 m s²

Cause of Upthrust

Pressure increases with depth, so the upward force on the bottom of an immersed object is greater than the downward force on the top. The resultant upward force is the upthrust:

FB − FT = hρgA = Vρg = upthrust
where h = height of object, A = cross-sectional area

2. Law of Flotation

The law of flotation states: a floating object displaces its own weight of the fluid in which it floats.

For a floating body: Upthrust = Weight of object  ⇒  Weight of fluid displaced = Weight of object
Example — Floating block

A wooden block of mass 500 g floats in water. What volume of water does it displace? (ρ_water = 1000 kg m³)

Solution
Weight of block = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N
Weight of water displaced = 5 N
Mass of water displaced = 5/10 = 0.5 kg
Volume displaced = 0.5/1000 = 5 × 10³ m³ = 500 cm³

3. Relative Density

Relative density = weight of substance / weight of equal volume of water
= weight in air / (weight in air − weight in water)
= mass of substance / mass of equal volume of water

Relative density has no units since it is a ratio. It is the same as specific gravity.

Example — Relative density

A stone weighs 25 N in air and 15 N when fully submerged in water. Find its relative density.

Solution
Upthrust = 25 − 15 = 10 N
Weight of equal volume of water = 10 N
Relative density = 25/10 = 2.5

4. Applications

ApplicationPrinciple used
Ships and boatsFloat because their average density is less than water (hollow hull displaces large volume)
SubmarinesAdjust buoyancy by taking in or expelling water from ballast tanks
BalloonsFilled with gas (H&sub2; or He) less dense than air — upthrust > weight
HydrometerMeasures relative density of liquids; sinks deeper in less dense liquids
LactometerTests purity of milk by measuring its relative density

Review Exercise

g = 10 m s²  |  ρwater = 1000 kg m³

1.

State Archimedes’ principle and the law of flotation.

2.

A body weighs 60 N in air and 45 N when immersed in water. Find: (a) upthrust, (b) relative density, (c) volume of object.

3.

A wooden block of volume 500 cm³ floats in water with 300 cm³ submerged. Find: (a) upthrust on the block, (b) mass of the block, (c) density of wood.

4.

(a) Explain why a ship made of steel floats even though steel is denser than water.

(b) Describe how a submarine dives and resurfaces.

5.

A hydrometer floats in water with 8 cm of its stem above the surface. When placed in a liquid of relative density 1.2, what length of stem is above the surface? (Hydrometer stem cross-section = 0.5 cm², volume of bulb = 40 cm³)
