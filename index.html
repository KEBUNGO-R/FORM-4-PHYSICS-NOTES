1. Production of X-rays

X-rays is an electromagnetic radiation discovered accidentally by Röntgen, a German physicist, while conducting research on cathode rays. X-rays owe their name to the fact that at the time of their discovery, their nature was unknown.

It is now known that X-rays are produced when fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped by matter.

X-rays are produced in X-ray tubes where fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped on hitting metal targets.

When a current flows through the filament in the cathode, electrons are produced by thermionic emission. The electrons are accelerated towards the target (anode) by the high potential difference of the order 100 kV between cathode and anode. The cathode is concave-shaped so as to focus the electron beam onto the target, which is usually tungsten embedded onto copper.

Most of the kinetic energy of the electron is converted to heat, with only about 0.5% being transformed to X-ray radiation. The target must therefore be a metal of high melting point, such as tungsten or molybdenum.

The anode is made of a good conductor of heat (copper) to ensure efficient dissipation of heat. Cooling is further enhanced by cooling fins on the outside and circulation of oil through the channels in the copper block.

The target is set at an angle (usually 45°) to the electron beam to direct the X-rays out through a window on the lead shield. X-rays at unsuitable angles are absorbed by the lead shield. The high voltage is supplied from a step-up transformer.

X-rays are produced in bursts — only during the half-cycles when the anode is positive with respect to the cathode. Due to the high frequency of a.c., the production appears continuous.

2. Properties of X-rays

  • iX-rays are not deflected by magnetic or electric fields — they carry no charge.
  • iiThey penetrate matter, with least penetration in high-density materials such as lead.
  • iiiThey affect photographic emulsions — the basis of X-ray photography.
  • ivThey ionise air molecules by knocking electrons off molecules in their path, increasing the conductivity of gases.
  • vThey cause photoelectric emission.
  • viThey cause fluorescence in certain substances, e.g. zinc sulphide.
  • viiThey are electromagnetic waves of shorter wavelength than visible light — they can be plane-polarised and diffracted.
  • viiiThey travel in straight lines at the speed of light (3.0 × 108 m s−1).

3. X-rays as Part of the Electromagnetic Spectrum

X-rays are electromagnetic waves of very short wavelength, of the order of 10−10 m. For classification purposes, electromagnetic waves in the range 10−11 m to 10−8 m are considered as X-rays.

This range overlaps with ultraviolet at the upper end and gamma rays at the lower end — the only difference being the manner of production.

4. Energy of X-rays

If a bombarding electron is stopped in a single collision, most or all of its energy is converted to X-ray energy.

The kinetic energy (K.E.) of the bombarding electron equals:

K.E. = eV where e = electron charge, V = accelerating potential difference

When all kinetic energy converts to X-ray energy, the frequency is maximum (fmax). Using Planck's theory:

E = hf eV = hfmax Since c = fλ  ⟹  λmin = hc / eV where h = Planck's constant, c = speed of light, e = electron charge

The most energetic X-rays have the shortest wavelength. Higher accelerating voltage → shorter λmin → more energetic X-rays.

Python — X-ray energy calculator (run in any Python environment)
# X-ray energy from accelerating voltage
h = 6.63e-34   # Planck's constant (J·s)
c = 3.0e8      # Speed of light    (m/s)
e = 1.6e-19    # Electron charge   (C)

V_kV = 100                         # Change this value
V    = V_kV * 1000

lam_min = (h * c) / (e * V)         # Minimum wavelength
f_max   = c / lam_min               # Maximum frequency
E_max   = h * f_max                 # Maximum photon energy

print(f"λ_min = {lam_min:.3e} m")
print(f"f_max = {f_max:.3e} Hz")
print(f"E_max = {E_max:.3e} J  ({E_max/e/1000:.1f} keV)")

6. Intensity (Quantity) and Quality of X-rays

The intensity of X-rays is controlled by the heating current. Greater heating current → more electrons produced → more X-rays (but same energy per photon).

The quality (strength/penetrating power) of X-rays depends on the speed of electrons, which depends on the accelerating potential difference. Higher p.d. → stronger, more penetrating X-rays.

Intensity ∝ Heating current (controls quantity)
Quality ∝ Accelerating voltage (controls energy per photon)

7. Uses of X-rays

FieldApplication
Medicine Radiography — diagnosing fractures, detecting foreign objects in the body (e.g. swallowed pins), imaging liver, spleen, kidneys after accidents.
Medicine Radiotherapy — CT examinations used to plan and administer radiation treatment for tumours. A point source of X-rays is required since X-rays cannot be focused by lenses.
Industry Detecting flaws in metal castings and welding joints. Also used to sterilise surgical equipment before packaging.
Crystallography Studying the crystal structure of substances (X-ray diffraction).
Security Airport baggage screening — inspecting luggage for hidden weapons or contraband.

8. Dangers of X-rays

Warning: Excessive exposure of living tissue to X-rays is dangerous — the radiation can damage or kill cells, and may cause cancer or genetic mutations.

Precautions taken:

  • X-ray machines have a protective lead shield to protect operators from stray radiation.
  • The machines are operated in rooms with thick concrete walls to absorb any leaking radiation.
  • Operators wear lead aprons and dosimeters to monitor exposure.
  • Patients are only exposed for the minimum necessary time.

9. Worked Examples

Constants: h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s  |  c = 3.0 × 108 m s−1  |  e = 1.6 × 10−19 C

Example 1

Find the frequency and the energy of X-rays whose wavelength is 10−10 m in a vacuum.

Solution

Using c = fλ:

f = c / λ = (3.0 × 108) / (10−10)
f = 3.0 × 1018 Hz

Using E = hf:

E = 6.63 × 10−34 × 3.0 × 1018
E = 1.989 × 10−15 J
Example 2

X-ray frequencies range from 3.0 × 1017 Hz to 3.0 × 1019 Hz. Determine: (a) the range of wavelengths, (b) the maximum energy.

Solution

(a) Wavelength range using λ = c / f:

λ1 = (3.0 × 108) / (3.0 × 1017) = 1.0 × 10−9 m
λ2 = (3.0 × 108) / (3.0 × 1019) = 1.0 × 10−11 m

Range: 10−11 m to 10−9 m

(b) Maximum energy using Emax = hfmax:

Emax = 6.63 × 10−34 × 3.0 × 1019
Emax = 1.989 × 10−14 J
Example 3

An X-ray tube has an accelerating potential difference of 100 kV. What is the shortest wavelength in its X-ray beam?

Solution

Using λmin = hc / eV:

λmin = (6.63 × 10−34 × 3.0 × 108) / (1.6 × 10−19 × 100 × 103)

λmin = (1.989 × 10−25) / (1.6 × 10−14)

λmin = 1.24 × 10−11 m
