1. Production of X-rays

X-rays is an electromagnetic radiation discovered accidentally by Röntgen, a German physicist, while conducting research on cathode rays.

It is now known that X-rays are produced when fast-moving electrons are suddenly stopped by matter.

When a current flows through the filament in the cathode, electrons are produced by thermionic emission. The electrons are accelerated towards the target (anode) by the high potential difference of the order 100 kV. The cathode is concave-shaped to focus the electron beam onto the target, which is usually tungsten embedded onto copper.

Most of the kinetic energy of the electron is converted to heat, with only about 0.5% transformed to X-ray radiation. The target must be a metal of high melting point, such as tungsten or molybdenum.

The target is set at an angle (usually 45°) to direct the X-rays out through a window in the lead shield. The high voltage is supplied from a step-up transformer. X-rays are produced in bursts — only during the half-cycles when the anode is positive.

2. Properties of X-rays

  • iX-rays are not deflected by magnetic or electric fields — they carry no charge.
  • iiThey penetrate matter, with least penetration in high-density materials such as lead.
  • iiiThey affect photographic emulsions — the basis of X-ray photography.
  • ivThey ionise air molecules by knocking electrons off molecules in their path, increasing the conductivity of gases.
  • vThey cause photoelectric emission.
  • viThey cause fluorescence in certain substances, e.g. zinc sulphide.
  • viiThey are electromagnetic waves of shorter wavelength than visible light — they can be plane-polarised and diffracted.
  • viiiThey travel in straight lines at the speed of light (3.0 × 108 m s−1).

3. X-rays as Part of the Electromagnetic Spectrum

X-rays are electromagnetic waves of very short wavelength, of the order of 10−10 m. For classification purposes, the range 10−11 m to 10−8 m is considered X-rays. This range overlaps with ultraviolet at the upper end and gamma rays at the lower end.

4. Energy of X-rays

The kinetic energy (K.E.) of the bombarding electron equals:

K.E. = eV where e = electron charge, V = accelerating potential difference

When all kinetic energy converts to X-ray energy, the frequency is maximum (fmax). Using Planck’s theory:

E = hf    eV = hfmax Since c = fλ  ⇒  λmin = hc / eV

The most energetic X-rays have the shortest wavelength.

6. Intensity (Quantity) and Quality of X-rays

The intensity of X-rays is controlled by the heating current. Greater heating current → more electrons → more X-rays.

The quality (penetrating power) depends on the accelerating potential difference. Higher p.d. → stronger X-rays.

Intensity ∝ Heating current (controls quantity)
Quality ∝ Accelerating voltage (controls energy per photon)

8. Dangers of X-rays

Warning: Excessive exposure can damage or kill cells and may cause cancer or genetic mutations.

Precautions:

  • Machines have a protective lead shield.
  • Operated in rooms with thick concrete walls.
  • Operators wear lead aprons and dosimeters.
  • Patients exposed for the minimum necessary time.

9. Worked Examples

Constants: h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s  |  c = 3.0 × 108 m s−1  |  e = 1.6 × 10−19 C

Example 1 — Find frequency and energy from wavelength

Find the frequency and energy of X-rays whose wavelength is 10−10 m.

Solution
f = c / λ = (3.0 × 108) / (10−10) = 3.0 × 1018 Hz
E = hf = 6.63 × 10−34 × 3.0 × 1018 = 1.989 × 10−15 J
Example 2 — Frequency range to wavelength range

X-ray frequencies range from 3.0 × 1017 Hz to 3.0 × 1019 Hz. Find (a) the wavelength range, (b) maximum energy.

Solution
λ1 = c/fmin = 3×108 / 3×1017 = 1.0 × 10−9 m
λ2 = c/fmax = 3×108 / 3×1019 = 1.0 × 10−11 m
Emax = hfmax = 6.63×10−34 × 3×1019 = 1.989 × 10−14 J
Example 3 — Minimum wavelength from accelerating voltage

An X-ray tube has an accelerating p.d. of 100 kV. Find the shortest wavelength.

Solution
λmin = hc / eV = (6.63×10−34 × 3×108) / (1.6×10−19 × 105) = 1.24 × 10−11 m
