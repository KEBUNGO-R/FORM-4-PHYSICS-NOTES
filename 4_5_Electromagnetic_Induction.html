1. Induced EMF and Faraday's Law

Electromagnetic induction is the production of an EMF (and hence current) in a conductor when there is relative motion between the conductor and a magnetic field.

Faraday’s Law

Faraday’s law states: the magnitude of the induced EMF in a circuit is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linkage through the circuit.

EMF = −N ΔΦ/Δt
where N = number of turns, ΔΦ = change in flux, Δt = time interval

Lenz’s Law

The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change causing it (conservation of energy).

Factors affecting the induced EMF: speed of motion, strength of magnetic field, number of turns in coil, and angle between conductor and field.

2. AC and DC Generators

AC Generator

A rectangular coil rotates in a magnetic field. The EMF induced alternates sinusoidally. Slip rings and brushes maintain continuous contact, delivering AC output.

DC Generator

Uses a split-ring commutator instead of slip rings. The commutator reverses connections every half revolution, producing a pulsating DC output.

EMF = NBAωsin(ωt)
Peak EMF: E&sub0; = NBAω
where N = turns, B = field strength, A = area, ω = angular velocity

3. Transformers

A transformer transfers electrical energy between circuits using electromagnetic induction. The iron core concentrates the magnetic flux.

Transformer equation: Vs/Vp = Ns/Np
For ideal transformer: VpIp = VsIs (power conserved)
Step-up: Ns > Np    Step-down: Ns < Np
Example 1 — Transformer calculation

A transformer has 500 primary turns and 2000 secondary turns. If primary voltage = 240 V, find the secondary voltage.

Solution
Vs/Vp = Ns/Np
Vs = Vp × Ns/Np = 240 × 2000/500
Vs = 960 V (step-up transformer)
Example 2 — Efficiency and current

A transformer steps 240 V down to 12 V. The secondary current is 5 A. Assuming 100% efficiency, find the primary current.

Solution
Pp = Ps  ⇒  VpIp = VsIs
Ip = VsIs / Vp = (12 × 5) / 240
Ip = 0.25 A

4. Power Transmission

Electrical power is transmitted at high voltage, low current to minimise energy losses in cables.

Power loss in cables: Ploss = I²R
High voltage → low current → less Ploss = I²R
Step-up transformer at power station, step-down at consumer end

This is why transformers are essential to the national grid: power stations generate at a few kilovolts, step up to 132 kV or 400 kV for long-distance transmission, then step down in stages to 240 V for homes.
