PHOTOELECTRIC EFFECT

Quantum theory of light, Einstein’s equation, factors affecting emission, and applications

15 Lessons·KEBUNGO-R Physics Notes

Specific Objectives — By the end of this topic, the learner should be able to:

  • Describe experiments to illustrate the photoelectric effect
  • Define threshold frequency, work function, and electron-volt
  • Explain photoelectric emission using Einstein’s equation: hf = hf0 + ½mv2
  • State factors affecting photoelectric emission
  • Describe applications: photo-emissive, photo-conductive, and photovoltaic cells
  • Solve numerical problems involving photoelectric emission

1. Light Energy and Quantum Theory

Max Planck (1901) proposed that light energy is propagated as small discrete packets called quanta or photons. The energy of one photon is:

E = hf
E = hc/λ
where h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s (Planck's constant), f = frequency, λ = wavelength

2. The Photoelectric Effect

Photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when electromagnetic radiation of sufficient frequency falls on it. The emitted electrons are called photoelectrons.

Key Terms

TermDefinition
Work function (φ)Minimum energy needed to remove an electron from the metal surface
Threshold frequency (f0)Minimum frequency of radiation that can eject electrons from a metal: φ = hf0
Electron-volt (eV)Energy gained by an electron through a potential difference of 1 V: 1 eV = 1.6 × 10−19 J
Stopping potential (Vs)Minimum voltage needed to stop the fastest photoelectrons: eVs = ½mv&sub2;max

3. Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

Einstein explained the photoelectric effect using quantum theory. When a photon of energy hf strikes an electron:

hf = φ + ½mvmax²
hf = hf0 + ½mvmax²
KEmax = ½mvmax² = hf − φ = h(f − f0)
where φ = work function, f0 = threshold frequency
Example 1 — Photon energy

Compare energies of red light (λ = 7.0 × 10−7 m) and violet light (λ = 4.0 × 10−7 m). h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s, c = 3.0 × 108 m s−1.

Solution
Ered = hc/λ = (6.63×10−34 × 3.0×108) / (7.0×10−7) = 2.84×10−19 J
Eviolet = hc/λ = (6.63×10−34 × 3.0×108) / (4.0×10−7) = 4.97×10−19 J
Violet light has higher frequency → higher photon energy
Example 2 — Einstein equation

Sodium has work function 3.0 × 10−19 J and threshold frequency 4.5 × 1014 Hz. Find the KE of photoelectrons emitted by light of frequency 6.0 × 1014 Hz.

Solution
KEmax = hf − φ
KEmax = 6.63×10−34 × 6.0×1014 − 3.0×10−19
KEmax = 3.978×10−19 − 3.0×10−19 = 9.78×10−20 J

4. Factors Affecting Photoelectric Emission

  • Frequency of radiation — must be above threshold frequency f0; higher frequency → greater KE of photoelectrons
  • Intensity of radiation — affects number of photoelectrons emitted (photoelectric current); does NOT affect KE of individual electrons
  • Type of metal — different metals have different work functions and threshold frequencies
Key point: Increasing intensity does NOT cause emission if frequency is below f0. Only frequency matters for whether emission occurs — intensity controls how many electrons are emitted.
↑ Back to top

5. Applications of Photoelectric Effect

  • Photo-emissive cells — used in exposure meters, burglar alarms, and TV cameras. Light falling on the cathode releases electrons which flow to the anode.
  • Photo-conductive cells (LDR) — resistance decreases when light falls on semiconductor material. Used in automatic street lights and camera exposure controls.
  • Photovoltaic cells (solar cells) — light directly generates an EMF. Used in solar panels, calculators, and spacecraft power systems.

Review Exercise

h = 6.63 × 10−34 J·s  |  c = 3.0 × 108 m s−1  |  e = 1.6 × 10−19 C  |  me = 9.1 × 10−31 kg

1.

(a) What is the photoelectric effect? (b) Define: work function, threshold frequency, stopping potential.

2.

Sodium has a work function of 3.7 × 10−19 J. Find: (a) threshold frequency, (b) maximum KE of electrons emitted by light of frequency 8.0 × 1014 Hz, (c) maximum speed of the photoelectrons.

3.

The stopping potential for photoelectrons from a metal is 2.5 V when illuminated by light of wavelength 2.0 × 10−7 m. Calculate: (a) photon energy, (b) work function, (c) threshold frequency.

4.

Explain why: (a) photoelectric emission does not occur below the threshold frequency regardless of intensity, (b) photoelectrons are emitted almost instantaneously when light above threshold frequency is incident.

5.

State three applications of the photoelectric effect and briefly explain how each one works.
