ELECTRONICS

Energy bands, semiconductors, doping, p-n junction diode, and rectification

1. Energy Band Theory

The behaviour of conductors, semiconductors, and insulators is explained by the energy band theory.

MaterialBand structureBand gapEffect of temperature
Conductor (e.g. copper)Valence and conduction bands overlapNoneResistance increases (more vibration hinders electrons)
Semiconductor (e.g. Si, Ge)Small gap between full valence band and empty conduction bandSmall (~1 eV)Resistance decreases (electrons can jump gap)
Insulator (e.g. glass)Large gap — conduction band always emptyLarge (~3–5 eV)No significant change

In semiconductors, when an electron jumps from the valence band to the conduction band, it leaves behind a hole (positive charge carrier). Both electrons and holes carry current.

2. Intrinsic and Extrinsic Semiconductors

Intrinsic Semiconductors

A pure semiconductor (e.g. pure silicon or germanium). At room temperature, a few electrons gain enough energy to jump to the conduction band, creating equal numbers of electrons and holes.

Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doping)

Doping is the deliberate addition of small amounts of impurity atoms to a pure semiconductor to increase its conductivity.

TypeDopantMajority carriersExample dopant
n-typePentavalent (5 valence electrons)Electrons (negative)Phosphorus (P), Arsenic (As)
p-typeTrivalent (3 valence electrons)Holes (positive)Boron (B), Aluminium (Al)

3. The p-n Junction Diode

When a p-type and n-type semiconductor are joined, a depletion region forms at the junction where mobile charge carriers have recombined. This creates an internal electric field called the contact potential (~0.6 V for silicon).

Forward Bias

Positive terminal connected to p-side, negative to n-side. The applied voltage opposes the contact potential. When it exceeds ~0.6 V (Si) or ~0.3 V (Ge), the depletion region narrows and current flows easily.

Reverse Bias

Positive terminal connected to n-side. The applied voltage adds to the contact potential. The depletion region widens and virtually no current flows (only a tiny reverse saturation current from minority carriers).

Forward bias: I ↑ (diode conducts)
Reverse bias: I ≈ 0 (diode blocks)
The diode acts as a one-way valve for current

4. Applications: Rectification

Half-Wave Rectification

A single diode allows only one half of the AC cycle to pass. The output is a pulsating DC with only positive (or negative) half cycles.

Circuit: AC source → diode → load resistor
Output: Half the AC waveform (pulsating DC) — 50% efficiency

Full-Wave Rectification (Bridge Rectifier)

Four diodes arranged in a bridge allow both halves of the AC cycle to contribute to the output. The output is pulsating DC at twice the frequency of the input.

Circuit: Four diodes in a bridge arrangement
Output: Full-wave pulsating DC — 100% efficiency
Smoothing: A capacitor in parallel with the load smooths the pulsating output to near-constant DC
FeatureHalf-waveFull-wave (bridge)
Diodes needed14
Output frequencySame as input (50 Hz)Double input (100 Hz)
Efficiency~40.6%~81.2%
RippleHigherLower (easier to smooth)
UseSimple, low-power circuitsMost power supplies

Review Exercise

1.

(a) Using the energy band theory, distinguish between conductors, semiconductors, and insulators.

(b) Why does the electrical resistance of a semiconductor decrease with increasing temperature?

2.

(a) What is doping? (b) Distinguish between n-type and p-type semiconductors, giving one example of a dopant for each.

(c) Draw a diagram of an n-type silicon crystal, indicating the extra (free) electron from the dopant.

3.

(a) Explain what happens when a p-n junction is: (i) forward biased, (ii) reverse biased.

(b) Sketch the current-voltage (I-V) characteristic curve for a p-n junction diode, labelling the forward bias, reverse bias, and breakdown regions.

4.

(a) Draw a circuit diagram for a half-wave rectifier. Sketch the input and output waveforms.

(b) Draw a circuit diagram for a full-wave bridge rectifier. How does adding a capacitor improve the output?

5.

A silicon diode is connected in series with a 2.2 kΩ resistor and a 5 V supply (forward bias). Calculate: (a) the voltage across the resistor, (b) the current in the circuit.
